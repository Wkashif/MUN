What is MUN?

Model United Nations (MUN) is an educational activity where students take on the role of representatives from different countries and discuss important global issues. It helps students understand how the world works and how nations communicate with one another to solve problems peacefully.

Flow of Debate

  1. Establish the Speaker's List-This list decides the order in which delegates will speak during the debate.
  2. Setting the Agenda-Each side briefly explains why their preferred topic is important, and the committee votes to select the agenda.
  3. Opening speeches-Delegates explain their country’s position on the chosen topic.
  4. Moderated Caucus-Delegates speak in a structured manner under the guidance of the Chair
  5. Unmoderated Caucus-Delegates move freely, discuss ideas, and work together to draft resolutions.
  6. Debate on Resolution-Delegates speak in favor or against the chosen resolution
  7. Debate on Amendments-Delegates speak in favor or against the chosen amendmendts
  8. Voting on amendments-Delegates vote on which amendment is made to the resolution
  9. Final Vote-Delegates vote on the final amended draft resolution

How to make a Resolution

A resolution is written in 3 main parts: the heading, the pre-ambulatory clauses, and the operative clauses.

Heading

The heading includes the committee name and the topic, along with other possible elements such as the main submitter, co-submitters, signatories, and sponsors. It is important to note that not every resolution contains all of these elements. Resolution guidelines are provided by the hosting institute, and based on these guidelines, delegates determine which details—beyond the committee name and topic—should be included in the heading.

  • Main submitter: the delegate that takes primary responsibility for drafting and presenting the resolution. This delegate leads the writing process, incorporates suggestions from co-submitters, and formally introduces the resolution to the committee during debate.
  • Co-submitter: a delegate that actively contributes to the drafting of a resolution and supports its content. Co-submitters work closely with the main submitter by offering ideas, edits, and amendments, and they formally endorse the resolution before it is presented to the committee.
  • Sponsor: a delegate that officially supports a resolution and agrees with its objectives and proposed solutions. Sponsors may have contributed to the drafting process, but their primary role is to show formal backing for the resolution and to advocate for its adoption during debate.
  • Signatory: a delegate that supports bringing a resolution to the floor for debate but does not necessarily agree with all of its content. Signatories sign the resolution to allow it to be introduced and discussed by the committee.

Pre-ambulatory clauses

Preambulatory clauses explain the background, context, and reasoning behind a resolution. They describe the current situation, reference past actions, international laws, treaties, resolutions, or concerns related to the topic, and justify why the resolution is necessary. These clauses set the foundation for the proposed solutions. Each clause begins with an italized pre-ambulatory phrase and ends with a comma.

Operative clauses

Operative clauses explain the actions and solutions proposed in a resolution. They state what the committee intends to do to address the issue. These clauses are written in numbered form, begin with underlined operative verbs, and end with a semicolon, except for the final clause, which ends with a period.Operative clauses may also include alphabetical sub-clauses (a, b, c, etc.) that provide further detail or break down actions into smaller steps.

*Please note that the resolution format, as well as the number of preambular and operative clauses, may vary in accordance with the hosting institute’s guidelines.*
